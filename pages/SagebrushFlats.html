TRAIL TIPS!

Sagebrush Flats appears as Trail 306/Soda Springs in many references, including the USDA site (and the book reference below). Nonetheless, Trail 306 proceeds from Soda Springs up to Sagebrush Flats at the location marked by the blue flag on the Actual Hike map (tooltips text is SSTH). The Soda Springs portion of the trail from Buckman Road is well marked, and the point at which the trail proceeds up to Sagebrush Flats is also marked. The second blue flag (tooltips text is SSFork) on the Actual Hike map indicates a fork from Trail 306 as it is drawn on area topo maps. Trail 306 presumably heads northeast at this point. The authors proceeded southwest along a well-worn trail, and intercepted Trail 306 shortly thereafter. This part of the trail may be a shortcut created by previous hikers. There is also a locally known landmark indicated by a yellow dot adjacent to the trail as it ascends the mesa, which may be the remnants of a meteor crash or perhaps small caldera. The authors did not visit the landmark as it was discovered after the hike was completed. The endpoint of this hike was the dropoff into Diablo Canyon. Following the arroyo to this point, it was not readily apparent how to proceed, and would have definitely required a high degree of care to descend. If the descent were made, one could proceed down the wash from Diablo Canyon to the original starting point, making for a shorter hike.

Sagebrush Flats is the name given to that part of Chino Mesa above Soda Springs. Soda Springs is actually a meadow area adjacent to the Rio Grande on the shore opposite of the Red Dot-Blue Dot Trail connector (shown as Silver Dot Trail on the map, but also called the River Trail). The trail from Buckman Road is well-marked and has little elevation change. From Buckman Road, Soda Springs is about 3.2 miles, where Trail 306 begins to ascend the mesa towards Sagebrush Flats, 800 feet up. It is somewhat steep at times, but generally well-traveled and easy to follow (see Trail Tips, above). Whether hiking towards Soda Springs, or ascending the mesa, or taking in the scenes from Sagebrush Flats, the views are excellent. The authors turned around at the point where the trail/arroyo drops down into Diablo Canyon (see Trail Tips).
