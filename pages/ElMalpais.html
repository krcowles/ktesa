El Malpais National Monument Index of Hikes

El Malpais is a vast area (590 square miles!) in western New Mexico, replete with lava fields, tubes, caves and other volcanic features. Many of the lava flows are quite young by geological standards, such as McCarty's flow at Lava Falls, dated at 3,000 years or less. Older flows of about 115,000 years can be seen in the El Calderon area. Because of the lava features' similarity to Hawaiian lava, many of the features bear Hawaiian names. In fact, El Malpais has been called "the poor man's Hawaii". Because of its size, there are numerous activities in which to engage, some planned by the park service, and others available to the public year-round. In addition to the hikes and scenic drives (partly along the Continental Divide), there are petroglyphs, old homesteads, and other cultural sites to explore. Star-gazing events, lectures, and Solstice Walks have been sponsored by the Park Service. Back country drives are available in good weather, traversing the length of the park - these requiring four-wheel drive and sufficient ground clearance.

El Malpais, located south of Grants, New Mexico, is bounded on the east by Highway 117, and on the north and west by Highway 53. Adjacent to El Malpais are the Zuni Mountains and also Mt. Taylor. Further travel on Highway 117 leads to El Morro National Monument. West of the badlands is Ramah Navajo reservation land, and on the east, Acoma reservation land.

Because of the numerous hikes available in the park, a list is provided below from which to select and view the options - many of which the authors have not yet attempted. As the authors learn more about the area, additional entries to the table will be provided, including homestead and petroglyph hikes. The current index is far from comprehensive! Some of the areas require great care due to the fact that the lava is very sharp and difficult to traverse, as well as having the characteristic that it can look the same in every direction, making it easy to get lost. A GPS system would be advised, depending on where one is hiking. Travel time from Corrales is a bit more than 1-1/2 hours, or more, depending on which part of the park is being visited. There are numerous online links to visit, and a good place to start might be the National Park Service's El Malpais site. Another good reference, providing historical and cultural context is the book El Malpais, Mt. Taylor, and the Zuni Mountains by Sherry Robinson.
